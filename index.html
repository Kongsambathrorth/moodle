3. üß© Moodle Activities & Resources

Activities are interactive features (Quizzes, Forums). Resources are static content (Files, Pages).

Essential Moodle Activities (Click to Flip!)

Assignment

Allows students to submit work (files or online text) for teacher grading. Used for essays, projects, or confirming offline tasks.

Quiz

Flexible activity for automated assessments (MC, True/False, Numerical). Grades are automatically recorded.

Forum

Enables asynchronous discussions over time. Used for social space, announcements (News Forum), Q&A, or extension activities.

H5P Activity

A content authoring plugin for interactive content (drag-and-drop, interactive video, games) directly within the course.

Lesson

Delivers content with "branching" logic. Directs learners to different pages based on their responses to embedded questions.

Workshop

A peer assessment activity where students submit work, and other students review and grade the submissions based on a teacher-defined criteria.

Essential Moodle Resources

Book

A multi-page resource with chapters and subchapters, useful for displaying lengthy information like departmental handbooks or reading material.

Page

Creates a simple web page resource using the text editor. Better for mobile than a file, ideal for course summaries or embedding multiple videos.

Folder

Displays a number of related files inside a single folder, reducing scrolling on the course page (e.g., a set of past exam papers).

URL

Provides a web link as a course resource. Can link to documents, images, YouTube videos, or external websites.

5. üìê ID Strategy: The ADDIE Model

The **ADDIE Model (Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, Evaluation)** is a five-phase framework used to manage and standardize the creation of training materials across RIF Academy. It ensures all courses are effective, measurable, and meet specific learning needs.

[Image of the ADDIE Model diagram]

5.1 Focus: The Design Phase (Content Blueprint)

The Design phase translates the goals identified in Analysis into a concrete blueprint. This is where you decide *how* the Moodle tools (Activities & Resources) will be used.

1. Define Learning Objectives (Goal Setting)

Start by defining clear, measurable, and concise objectives (Tip 2). These objectives directly guide your selection of Moodle activities and assessment strategies.

2. Sequence the Content and Activities (Flow)

Choose an appropriate content sequence (Chronological, Topical, Known-to-Unknown, etc.) (Tip 3). Map out where Moodle **Pages** introduce concepts and where **Quizzes/Assignments** apply them.

3. Create the Course Storyboard (Moodle Mapping)

Map out the contents of every section (Module/Topic). Define the type of Moodle Activity (Forum, Assignment, H5P) and the media type (video, text, image) for each lesson. Review this blueprint critically to ensure a variety of activities is included (Tip 14).

4. Determine Assessment Strategy

Decide whether to use Moodle's built-in tools (Quiz, Workshop) or external tools for scoring. If using the **Assignment** activity, plan to use a **Rubric** for transparent, multi-criteria assessment.

Practical Example: Gender Equity Awareness Blueprint

Content Blueprint: Gender Equity Awareness for Microfinance Staff

**Course Goal:** Equip microfinance staff with foundational awareness and tools to recognize and address gender biases.

6. üí° Engagement and Retention Techniques

This module introduces essential pedagogical and design techniques to maximize learner retention and participation in your Moodle courses.

Conversational & Pacing Strategies

Effective pacing and conversational language are vital for maintaining focus in an online environment.

Use Conversational Tone (Tip 16)

Address learners directly using "you" and "I" (first-person language). Research shows students work harder when they feel they are in a conversation.

Master the 90-20-8 Rule (Tip 20)

Keep lessons under 90 minutes. Change the pace or method every **20 minutes**. Involve the learner in an activity (poll, quiz, discussion) every **8 minutes** to ensure active participation.

Set the Tone with Icebreakers (Tip 18)

Use the Moodle **Forum** activity for a lighthearted, non-academic icebreaker at the start of the course to foster a friendly atmosphere and build community.

Use Humor (Tip 17) & Reminders (Tip 19)

Incorporate humor (memes, funny examples) to reduce stress. Use Moodle's Announcement or Email features to provide a clear "to-do list" and remind students of upcoming priorities.

Content for Retention (Storytelling & Practice)

Focus your energy on complexity and use active learning techniques to ensure knowledge application.

Apply the 80/20 Rule to Complexity (Tip 7)

Identify the 20% of concepts that require 80% of the effort. Dedicate more content, examples, and Moodle quiz attempts to these complex topics.

Provide Worked Out Examples (Tip 31)

Provide detailed, step-by-step explanations for novice learners. As they advance, make explanations shorter and available only on-demand (e.g., using a hidden Moodle **Page** resource).

Use Stories and Case Studies (Tips 30, 38)

Use the Moodle **Assignment** or **Database** activity to present real-life **Case Studies** that develop critical analysis and decision-making skills.

Design Authentic Activities (Tip 43) & Promote Practice (Tip 36)

Ensure activities require learners to apply knowledge in a meaningful way that has value beyond the course. Use Moodle **Workshop** (for peer review) or **Assignment** activities frequently for demonstration of skill improvement.

Visual Design & Feedback Loop

The visual elements and feedback mechanisms on Moodle directly impact focus and course quality.

Prioritize Distraction-Free Design (Tip 21)

Stick to a clean, simple Moodle theme. Remove all unnecessary side-blocks, graphics, and elements that do not directly support the learning goal (weeding).

Use Meaningful Images (Tip 22) & Consistent Color (Tips 24, 25)

Prefer photos over clip art. Select powerful images that evoke positive emotions for better retention. When using Moodle's Text and Media areas, use a consistent color palette (2-3 colors max) for visual simplicity.

Encourage Motivation (Tip 46) & Feedback (Tip 12)

Use Moodle's **Badges** or **Certificates** for extrinsic motivation. Gather **mid-course** feedback (using the Moodle **Feedback** activity) to adjust content and demonstrate responsiveness to learners.

Create the End-of-Course Experience (Tip 41)

Help learners recall the most essential concepts by asking a final reflection question, such as: "If you remember nothing else, remember **THIS** concept."

7. ‚ôø Course Accessibility: Enhancing Readability

Ensuring your Moodle course content is accessible is mandatory. This includes designing for keyboard navigation, screen readers, and learners with cognitive needs. This module focuses on the **Reading Mode** feature to enhance readability for text-heavy resources.

7.1 Understanding Reading Mode

Reading Mode globally changes the aesthetic properties of the page to prioritize text clarity over design complexity. This is especially useful for consuming large amounts of text (e.g., Moodle **Page** or **Book** resources).

High-Contrast Palette

Switches the background to a soft cream/yellow color and the text to true black, which reduces screen glare and improves focus compared to stark white backgrounds.

Enhanced Typography

The font switches to a serif typeface with increased size and line spacing, making long passages easier to track and read (a key recommendation from accessibility experts).

Usage:

To activate this mode, click the **"Aa"** button next to the main menu toggle. Encourage learners to use it when reading text-heavy materials.

7.2 Moodle Content Design for Accessibility

Beyond reading mode, remember to format content correctly within Moodle:

  • **Use Moodle's built-in heading styles** (Heading 2, 3, 4) in the text editor for proper screen reader navigation.
  • **Provide Alt Text** for all images you upload (Tip 22).
  • **Ensure all videos include captions** (Moodle supports this via video embed options).

8. üì¶ SCORM Explained

SCORM (Sharable Content Object Reference Model) is a set of technical standards that allows eLearning content to be shared across different Learning Management Systems (LMS). It ensures compatibility and progress tracking for highly interactive, externally built content.

What is a SCORM Package?

A SCORM package is a collection of files (usually packaged as a ZIP file) created by authoring tools (like Articulate Storyline or Adobe Captivate). It acts as a common language that Moodle understands, allowing interactive multimedia content and assessments to be uploaded and tracked.

When to use the SCORM Activity in Moodle

  • **Interactive Multimedia:** For presenting complex animations, simulations, or scenarios created outside of Moodle's native editor.
  • **Automated Assessment:** When content includes detailed, scored questions that must be tracked automatically (SCORM reports the score directly to the Moodle Gradebook).
  • **Interoperability:** If you need to reuse content created for another LMS platform.

Procedure: Uploading Rise 360 SCORM to Moodle

Follow these steps in Moodle to deploy your Rise 360 output ZIP file:

Video Placeholder: How to upload a SCORM package in Moodle Activity

Step 1: Locate SCORM Activity

In your Moodle course, click **"Turn editing on"**, then **"Add an activity or resource,"** and select **"SCORM package."**

Step 2: Upload ZIP File

Under the **"Package"** section, drag and drop the **Rise 360 output ZIP file** into the designated file area.

Step 3: Configure Appearance (Optional)

Under **"Appearance,"** choose how the content displays (e.g., **New window** or **Current window**). This affects the user experience.

Step 4: Configure Grade (Important)

Under **"Grade,"** verify the maximum grade, grading method, and ensure the score is passed correctly from the SCORM package to the Moodle Gradebook.

Step 5: Save and Display

Click **"Save and display."** Moodle will launch the package, allowing you to test the scoring and navigation.

SCORM vs. H5P (Important Distinction)

While both create interactive content:

  • **SCORM** requires external authoring software and is uploaded as a ZIP file. It is the industry standard for formal tracking.
  • **H5P** is a Moodle plugin that allows you to build interactive content directly inside Moodle using your browser. It's simpler for quick, native interactions.
